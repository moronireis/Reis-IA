## USAGE NOTES

- Every post starts with a strong first line — this is what appears before "see more." It determines whether anyone reads the rest.
- One idea per post. Never two.
- Line breaks after every 1–2 sentences. Dense paragraphs are invisible on LinkedIn.
- End with a question or a direct CTA — never let the post just stop.
- Do not use hashtags in the body copy. If used at all, limit to 2–3 and place them at the very end.
- Optimal post length: 150–300 words. Some contrarian/thought leadership posts can be shorter (50–100 words). Long-form educational posts can go to 400–500 words.
- Recommended posting frequency: 5 posts/week, using a rotating mix of the types below.

### Template 3A — "Here's the Framework"

**Hook:**
How to run an AI Revenue Audit on your own business in 3 steps:

(This is the first thing we do with every client — before writing a line of code.)

**Step 1: Map your revenue levers.**
Where does money come from in your business? Lead generation, conversion, retention, operational margin? Pick the top two. AI should connect to one of them.

**Step 2: Identify the friction.**
What's slowing down or leaking revenue in those two areas? Slow lead response? Manual qualification? Repetitive support? That friction is where AI creates leverage.

**Step 3: Define the measurement.**
Before building anything, define what "working" looks like. More qualified leads per month? Reduction in support ticket volume? Faster response time? If you can't measure it, you can't improve it.

Most companies skip Step 3.

That's why they can't measure AI ROI — and why 29% of executives say they have no idea if their AI investments are producing anything.

Outcome defined first. Technology chosen second. Always.

### Template 3B — "The 3 Mistakes"

**Hook:**
Three mistakes agencies make when adding AI to their services:

**Mistake 1: Leading with the technology.**
"We build AI chatbots" is not a value proposition. It's a capability. Lead with the outcome: "We reduce your sales team's time on unqualified leads by 60%." Same technology. Different conversation.

**Mistake 2: Pricing by scope instead of value.**
If you price AI projects by hours or features, you'll always undercharge. The right pricing anchor is business impact. A system that generates $50K in additional revenue per year is worth $10K to implement. Do the math with your client, not instead of them.

**Mistake 3: Delivering without measuring.**
Every AI implementation should have a defined KPI from day one. If there's no measurement framework, you can't prove the value — and you can't charge for it next time.

Most agencies make all three. The ones that fix them are the ones charging $15K–$20K per project instead of $3K.
