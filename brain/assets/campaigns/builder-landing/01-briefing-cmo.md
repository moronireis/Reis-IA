## CRITICAL CONTEXT: This Is NOT the Agency Builder Page

The existing `sales-page-builder.md` targets agency owners who want to sell AI services to their clients. THIS campaign targets a completely different buyer: **CEOs and founders of Brazilian companies (R$5-50M revenue) who want to implement AI as a revenue strategy inside their own business.** The Builder mentorship here teaches them to become strategic AI leaders within their company — not to sell AI as a service.

## A. ICP Definition for This Campaign

### The Decision Maker

- **Title**: CEO, founder, or managing partner (occasionally COO with full P&L authority)
- **Company**: Brazilian B2B company, R$5M-R$50M annual revenue
- **Team size**: 10-100 employees
- **Industries**: Professional services, SaaS, e-commerce, education, real estate, health/wellness, logistics
- **Age**: 32-50
- **Profile**: Data-driven operator. Built the company from scratch or led it through a significant growth phase. Comfortable with numbers and strategic decision-making. Not a technologist — delegates technical execution.
- **Budget authority**: R$20K-R$80K range for strategic initiatives (equivalent to $4K-$15K USD)
- **Decision style**: Needs to see the logic, the economics, and the risk mitigation. Not an impulse buyer. Will consult with 1-2 trusted advisors before committing.

### Awareness Level

**Problem-aware transitioning to solution-aware.**

They know their operations cannot keep up with growth. They know AI is relevant. Many have already attempted AI projects (chatbots, automations, internal tools) that either failed outright or delivered underwhelming results. They are skeptical of AI vendors but still believe AI holds strategic value — if approached differently.

Key nuance: They are NOT evaluating "AI vs. no AI." They are evaluating "try again vs. give up on AI for now." The landing page must address the re-engagement psychology of a burned buyer.

### Primary Pain (The Bleeding Neck)

"We invested in AI — time, money, team attention — and got nothing measurable in return. Now the board is asking about AI strategy, competitors are pulling ahead, and I don't know whether to try again or cut losses. The last thing I need is another technology project that produces a demo nobody uses."

This is a compound pain:
1. **Financial wound**: Money already spent with no ROI
2. **Credibility wound**: The CEO championed AI internally and it did not deliver
3. **Competitive wound**: Competitors who "figured it out" are gaining ground
4. **Decision paralysis**: Fear of making the same mistake twice

### Secondary Pains

- Operations cannot scale with current headcount and processes
- Sales team drowning in unqualified leads (manual qualification bottleneck)
- Support costs growing faster than revenue
- Key-person dependency across critical workflows
- Data exists but is not being used for decision-making
- Basic automations (Zapier, Make.com) hit their ceiling

### What They Have Already Tried

1. **Hired an AI agency or freelancer** — Got a prototype or chatbot. It "worked" in demos but never integrated properly into real workflows. Quietly abandoned after 3-6 months.
2. **Internal initiative** — Assigned the IT team or a tech-savvy employee to "explore AI." Resulted in experiments with no business framework. No one could answer "what is the ROI?"
3. **No-code/low-code automations** — Zapier, Make.com, basic chatbots. Helped with small tasks but did not create the strategic impact they expected from AI.
4. **AI consultants** — Received a strategy deck with recommendations but no execution support. Paid for a PDF that sits in a folder.

### Why Those Attempts Failed (Our Diagnosis)

Every failed attempt shares the same root cause: **they started with the technology instead of the revenue opportunity.** No one asked "where does AI make us more money?" before building. The Revenue-First AI Framework exists to fix this exact failure mode.

### Buying Triggers (What Makes Them Act NOW)

- Board meeting or investor call demanding an AI strategy
- Missed quarterly revenue target due to operational bottleneck
- Key employee quit, exposing process fragility and dependency
- Competitor deployed AI visibly (AI-powered customer experience, faster delivery)
- Read a report or attended an event that re-ignited AI urgency
- Realized current growth rate is unsustainable without operational leverage

### Objections (In Order of Intensity)

1. **"We tried AI before and it did not work."** (Highest — this is the wall to break through)
2. **"How do I know this approach is different?"** (Needs proof of methodology, not just claims)
3. **"Can you guarantee ROI?"** (Wants risk mitigation, not empty promises)
4. **"How long before we see results?"** (Impatient — has already waited and lost time)
5. **"Will it work with our systems?"** (Integration anxiety from past failures)
6. **"I do not have time for a 12-week program."** (CEO time is the scarcest resource)
7. **"Why a mentorship and not a done-for-you implementation?"** (Needs to understand the strategic value of learning to lead AI internally)

## D. Creative Direction

### Tone for This Piece

**Consultivo premium** — This is not a sales page. It reads like a strategic partner presenting a thesis. Think: the CEO opens this page and feels like they are reading a briefing from a trusted advisor, not being sold to by a vendor.

Specific tone markers:
- **Confident, not aggressive**: State facts and outcomes. No hype, no exclamation marks, no urgency tricks.
- **Empathetic without being soft**: Acknowledge the pain of failed AI investments directly. Do not dance around it. But do not dwell — pivot quickly to the framework and the path forward.
- **Specific, not abstract**: Every claim should have a number, a timeframe, or a concrete scenario. "42% of companies abandoned AI projects" not "many companies struggle with AI."
- **Peer-to-peer**: Moroni speaks as a fellow business strategist, not as a service provider pitching upward. The relationship is lateral, not hierarchical.

### What NOT to Say (Anti-Examples)

| Do NOT Write | Why | Write Instead |
|-------------|-----|---------------|
| "AI revolution" / "disruptive" / "game-changing" | AI hype triggers skepticism in burned buyers | "AI as a revenue strategy" / "measurable AI implementation" |
| "We guarantee X% ROI" | Unverifiable promise; damages credibility with sophisticated buyers | "Our framework is built to identify and measure ROI before implementation begins" |
| "Simple" / "easy" / "effortless" | CEOs know nothing worthwhile is effortless; diminishes perceived value | "Structured" / "methodical" / "designed for busy executives" |
| Any mention of APIs, code, prompts, models, LLMs | This is a C-level page, not a technical buyer page | Talk about business outcomes, revenue processes, operational capacity |
| "Join our community" / "network with peers" | Community is a bonus, not a selling point for this ICP | "Direct access to Moroni and the Revenue-First methodology" |
| Generic testimonials without specifics | "Great experience" means nothing to a skeptical CEO | Use specific scenarios: industry, problem, metric, timeframe |
| Pricing tables, tier comparisons, self-serve checkout | PERMANENT RULE: No SaaS patterns. This is a premium consultancy. | Single CTA: book a conversation at /agendar |
| Emojis, excessive formatting, visual noise | Violates brand voice: Apple, Porsche, Stripe aesthetic | Clean, confident, minimal. Every word earns its place. |

### Copy References That Work for This Audience

- **McKinsey / BCG executive briefings**: How they frame a problem, present data, and propose an approach. The structure, not the corporate jargon.
- **Stripe's marketing pages**: Clean, confident, specific. Every line communicates value without shouting.
- **Hormozi offer framing (applied to premium)**: The value equation logic — show the dream outcome, increase perceived probability, reduce perceived time and effort.
- **Porsche marketing**: Understated confidence. Never begs. Presents the object and lets quality speak.

### Visual Direction Notes (for designer-agent downstream)

- Dark background, minimal layout
- Large, confident typography (Inter)
- Muted gold accent only on CTAs and key data points
- Chess motif (strategy) in hero or section divider

## F. Offer Framing for This Page

### What We Are Selling on This Page

We are NOT selling the Builder program directly. We are selling a **strategic conversation** — a 30-45 minute call where Moroni diagnoses the CEO's AI situation and determines if the Revenue-First approach fits their business.

The landing page's job is to:
1. Make the CEO feel understood (pain validation)
2. Show there is a different approach (Revenue-First Framework)
3. Create enough curiosity and credibility to book the call

### What Happens After Booking

1. CEO books at /agendar (Cal.com)
2. Moroni conducts a diagnostic conversation (30-45 min)
3. If fit exists, Moroni presents the Builder mentorship tailored to their situation
4. Enrollment into the appropriate Builder tier (Group, Premium, or VIP)

The page does NOT need to explain tiers, pricing, or program modules. That happens on the call.

### Guarantee Adaptation for This ICP

The existing guarantee ("Build Your Offer or Your Money Back") is for agencies. For CEOs, adapt to:

**"Identify Revenue or We Keep Working"**: If the first phase of the program does not identify at least 3 concrete AI revenue opportunities in your business with projected ROI, we continue working at no additional cost until it does.

This shifts the risk: the CEO pays for the outcome (revenue identification), not for the process (12 weeks of calls). Present this guarantee concept on the page as risk reversal, but keep it brief — details happen on the call.

## G. Strategic Notes for Copywriter

1. **The emotional sequence is: validation > diagnosis > mechanism > path forward.** Do not start with the offer. Start with "we know what happened."

2. **The enemy is the approach, not the technology.** AI is not the villain. The way companies implement AI (tech-first instead of revenue-first) is the villain. This distinction matters — the CEO still believes in AI's potential.

3. **Specificity wins everything.** "42% of companies abandoned AI projects" beats "many companies struggle with AI" every time. Use the data points from the research.

4. **The CEO is buying TIME and CLARITY, not AI implementation.** The real transformation: from "confused and burned by AI" to "in control with a clear revenue strategy." Frame accordingly.

5. **Avoid the "course" frame entirely.** This is not a course. This is not training. This is a strategic mentorship where a business strategist helps the CEO turn AI into a revenue function. Language matters: "mentorship" or "program", never "course" or "training" or "workshop."

6. **One CTA, one action: /agendar.** Every section should naturally funnel toward booking. No secondary CTAs, no downloads, no "learn more" links. The page exists to fill the calendar.

7. **Moroni is the product.** At this price point and for this audience, the CEO is buying access to Moroni's strategic thinking and methodology. The page should position Moroni as the peer strategist, not as a vendor or instructor.
