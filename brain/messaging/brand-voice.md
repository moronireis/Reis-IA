## Voice Identity

Reis IA sounds like **a sharp business strategist who happens to be deeply technical** — not the other way around. The voice leads with business insight and uses technology as the supporting evidence, never as the headline.

---

## Voice Characteristics (5 Adjectives)

1. **Direct** — Says what needs to be said without hedging. No filler, no fluff.
2. **Confident** — Speaks from experience, not theory. Backs claims with data or examples.
3. **Pragmatic** — Focused on what works, not what sounds impressive. Real-world over academic.
4. **Energetic** — Carries momentum and urgency without being frantic. There's a sense that something is happening.
5. **Accessible** — Makes complex AI concepts feel simple and actionable. Never talks down to the audience.

## Vocabulary

### Words and Phrases TO USE
- Revenue, profit, ROI, results
- Implement, deploy, build, ship
- Revenue-First (our framework name)
- System, engine, infrastructure
- Scale, grow, multiply
- Ecosystem, partnership, collaboration
- "Here's what's working..."
- "The real question is..."
- "Let me show you..."
- "This is what most people get wrong..."
- Practical, actionable, proven
- Opportunity, advantage, edge

### Words and Phrases TO AVOID
- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive" (overused AI hype)
- "Cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art" (generic tech jargon)
- "Leverage" as a verb (corporate-speak)
- "Synergy," "paradigm shift," "thought leader" (empty buzzwords)
- "Just" or "simply" when describing implementation (minimizes the work)
- "AI will replace..." (fear-based messaging)
- "Magic," "overnight," "effortless" (unrealistic promises)
- "Perhaps," "maybe," "we think" (uncertainty — be specific or don't say it)
- Excessive technical acronyms without explanation (LLM, RAG, NLP — explain if the audience is business owners)

## Style Rules

### Sentence Structure
- Lead with the point, then explain. Never bury the lead.
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones. Rhythm matters.
- Use questions to engage: "Here's what most agencies miss:" > "It is important to consider that..."
- One idea per paragraph in social content. Dense paragraphs lose readers.

### Reading Level
- Target: 8th-10th grade reading level for all customer-facing content
- Exception: Proposals and technical documentation can go higher
- Simple words > complex words. "Use" > "utilize." "Start" > "commence."

### Numbers and Data
- Use specific numbers whenever possible. "$47,000 in new revenue" > "significant revenue increase."
- Reference market data to back claims. The research file has strong statistics.
- Round numbers are fine for approximation. Exact numbers are better for case studies.

### Formatting
- Use bullet points and headers for scannability
- Bold key phrases in long-form content
- Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences in social content
- Use line breaks liberally — dense text blocks are the enemy

## Do / Don't Examples

### Example 1: Describing Reis IA

**Do**: "We build AI systems that make businesses money. Not prototypes. Not demos. Production systems that generate revenue from day one."

**Don't**: "We are a cutting-edge AI solutions provider leveraging state-of-the-art technology to deliver transformative digital experiences for forward-thinking enterprises."

### Example 2: Talking About AI

**Do**: "AI is not complicated. The hard part is knowing where it creates the most value in your specific business. That's what we figure out first."

**Don't**: "Our proprietary AI algorithms utilize advanced natural language processing and machine learning pipelines to optimize business processes through intelligent automation."

### Example 3: Addressing Objections

**Do**: "You tried AI before and it didn't work? That's actually the most common thing we hear. The problem wasn't the technology — it was the approach. Starting with the tech instead of the business outcome is why 42% of AI projects fail. We start with the revenue opportunity."

**Don't**: "We understand your concerns about AI implementation. Our team of experts will ensure a seamless integration process with minimal disruption to your operations."

### Example 4: Making an Offer

**Do**: "The Builder program runs for 12 weeks. You'll walk out with a sellable AI offer, a client acquisition system, and your first project delivered. If you do the work and don't have a sellable offer by the end, you get your money back."

**Don't**: "Our comprehensive mentorship program offers an extensive curriculum covering various aspects of AI services development, designed to empower agency leaders with the knowledge and tools needed to thrive in the evolving digital landscape."

### Example 5: LinkedIn Post

**Do**: "Most agencies will lose clients to AI-enabled competitors this year. Not because AI is hard. Because they're waiting for the perfect time to start. There is no perfect time. There's starting, or there's watching."

**Don't**: "Excited to announce that AI is transforming the agency landscape! Here are 5 reasons why agencies should consider integrating AI into their service offerings..."

## How Moroni Should Sound in Content

Moroni's personal voice is the brand voice amplified with these additional elements:

- **Builder identity**: Moroni builds things. Content should reflect someone who is actively doing, not just teaching or theorizing. Share what you're building, what you're testing, what you're learning.
- **Strategic thinker**: Moroni sees patterns and opportunities others miss. Content should demonstrate this through unique angles and contrarian takes.
- **Generous with knowledge**: Share real frameworks, real numbers, real processes. The transparency builds trust and differentiates from gurus who gatekeep everything.
- **Honest about the grind**: Don't pretend everything is easy or guaranteed. Acknowledge the work involved. This builds credibility and attracts serious buyers.
- **Brazilian perspective**: Moroni brings a unique lens as someone operating at the intersection of the Brazilian and global markets. This is an asset, not a limitation.

## Channel-Specific Adaptations

### LinkedIn
- Open with a hook (first line matters most)
- Use line breaks between every sentence
- End with a question or clear CTA
- Share personal opinions and hot takes — LinkedIn rewards conviction

### YouTube
- Speak directly to camera
- Start with "Here's what you need to know about..." or "Most people get this wrong..."
- Use b-roll and screen recordings to show, not just tell
- End every video with a specific next step

### Email
- Write like you're texting a colleague — short, direct, valuable
- Subject lines: curiosity-driven or benefit-driven. Never clickbait.
- One CTA per email. One.
- P.S. lines work — use them for secondary offers or social proof.

### Instagram
- Reels: First 3 seconds must hook. State the problem or contrarian take immediately.
- Stories: Behind-the-scenes, polls, quick tips. Casual and authentic.
- Captions: Short and punchy. Save the long-form for LinkedIn.
