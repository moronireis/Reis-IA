## REIS [IA] Aesthetic Intent

We want a home that reads like a consulting firm with a cinematographer's eye: dark as default (not as costume), architectural in its grid discipline, and confident enough to let a single electric blue accent carry the entire emotional register. The fifteen references below each contribute one precise slice of that ambition — Linear and Vercel teach us how to breathe in the dark with editorial calm; Apple and Porsche teach us cinematic gravity and how a hero can feel inevitable rather than decorative; Stripe, Ramp and Mercury teach us the restraint of consulting authority, the way a grid becomes the argument; Raycast and Cursor show the pro-tool precision we want in denser sections where the executive reader expects substance; NYT and Bloomberg Graphics anchor the long-form scroll rhythm for our methodology chapters; Bureau Borsche and Pentagram hold the line on Swiss editorial typography so our Inter-only stack earns its premium weight. The emotional target is unambiguous: executive confidence, cinematic gravity, editorial precision, zero noise. Nothing ornate, nothing playful, nothing that could belong to another AI startup. If a reference feels clever, we leave it. If it feels inevitable, we harvest it.

## Cluster 1 — Dark Editorial Premium

This cluster teaches us how to live in the dark without collapsing into the generic "SaaS black". The hallmark is atmospheric depth — soft radial glows, near-black backgrounds that shift subtly by section, and typography that is given enough air to function as the primary ornament. What to steal: the confident whitespace, the way an accent color appears maybe three times on an entire page and each appearance is earned. What to leave behind: the product-launch density, the feature grid reflex, anything that smells of startup-in-a-hurry.

### Ref 1.1 — Linear
- **URL**: https://linear.app
- **Cluster**: Dark Editorial Premium
- **Why it's good**: The home is a masterclass in dark-mode hierarchy — every section has its own atmospheric pressure, held together by a disciplined vertical rhythm that never rushes. Type sizes step down with almost musical intervals and the accent hues appear sparingly, which makes each appearance feel like punctuation rather than decoration. The overall soul is quiet authority: it trusts the reader to lean in, and because it does, the reader does. The composition treats whitespace as a premium material.
- **Tags**: #dark-editorial #vertical-rhythm #confident-whitespace #accent-restraint
- **Harvest priority**: HIGH
- **Harvest risk**: low

### Ref 1.2 — Vercel

## Notes for design-system-extractor

- **Apple, Porsche, Polestar, Rivian**: heavy JavaScript SPAs with aggressive anti-bot and bot-detection layers. Treat as mood-only references — commission original imagery instead of attempting harvest. If a harvest is attempted, expect empty HTML shells and lazy-loaded media that will not resolve in a static fetch.
- **NYT interactive features**: paywalled and geo-restricted in parts, with session-gated content. Low harvest feasibility.
- **Bloomberg Graphics**: partial paywall and heavy client-side rendering of data figures. Harvest the shell, not the figures.
- **Arc Browser (arc.net)**: uses client-side rendered chapters; the initial HTML may be thin. If the static fetch returns a near-empty document, fall back to screenshot-based reference and extract the type scale and spacing manually.
- **Framer marketing pages**: self-referentially built in Framer — markup is auto-generated and not always clean. Prefer to extract type scale and spacing tokens rather than component markup.
- **Fallback chain**: if Linear fails, fall back to Vercel; if Stripe fails, fall back to Mercury; if Raycast fails, fall back to Cursor. The Top 10 is designed so that any single failure still leaves the cluster intent covered by a sibling reference.
- **What to prioritize in each harvest**: type scale (px ladder and line-height), section vertical rhythm (padding tokens), accent usage frequency (how often the brand color actually appears per 1000px of scroll), and dark-mode layering (how many distinct near-black tones are used per page). These are the transferable lessons — not the specific component markup.
